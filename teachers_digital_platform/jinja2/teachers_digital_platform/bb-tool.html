Take a tour of the building blocks

As children grow, their ability to manage money and understand financial concepts grows with them. Financial capability stems from three interconnected building blocks of youth development. Understand how the building blocks work together to help young people achieve financial capability and learn strategies for teaching them in your classroom.

Adult financial well-being

The ultimate goal of the work of BCFP is to help you improve your financial well-being. Financial well-being means how much your financial situation and money choices provide you with security and freedom of choice.

Youth financial capability

Developing financial capability during youth is an important stepping stone on the path to adult financial well-being. Financially capable individuals have healthy money habits, stick to plans, and apply their knowledge and skills to successfully complete financial tasks. These knowledge, skills, and behaviors of financial capability stem from three interconnected building blocks that develop over time and are marked by key milestones.

Building blocks of youth financial capability

The building blocks provide a framework for youth financial literacy education and help students learn the skills and knowledge they need to achieve financial well-being as adults. Children and youth need all three of the interconnected building blocks to achieve adult financial capability.

Executive function Financial habits and norms Financial knowledge and decision-making skills
What is this building block? Executive function is the ability to plan ahead, remember information, multitask, solve problems, and control impulses. These are key skills for money management. Financial habits and norms are the values, standards, routine practices, and rules of thumb you use to navigate your daily financial activities. Financial knowledge and decision-making skills include familiarity with financial facts and concepts along with competency in research and analysis.
Why is it important? Executive function lays the foundation for financial capability. The associated skills help you save money, set financial goals, make buying and saving decisions, stick to a budget, and resist impulse buys. Financial habits and norms help guide your day-to-day behaviors and money management routines for spending and saving. They shape the way you carry out common financial tasks. Financial knowledge and decision-making skills help you find credible consumer information and manage your money wisely—so you can make informed financial decisions.
When do young people develop it? Development of this building block begins during early childhood (ages 3-5) and builds in complexity throughout development, as a child’s brain evolves and their cognitive abilities mature. Executive function continues to develop into adulthood. During middle childhood (ages 6-12), this building block becomes more well-developed through observation and direct experiences with money. Financial habits and norms continue to evolve through adolescence and into adulthood. During adolescence (13-21), development of this building block is at its height as young people have more direct experiences with money and the financial world and are more engaged in complex tasks and decision making. Financial knowledge and decision making continues to develop into adulthood.
How is it fostered in the classroom? Effective strategies for fostering executive function allow students to gain exposure to and explore and practice these attitudes and skills. Effective strategies for fostering financial habits and norms allow youth to draw meaning and connections from hands-on and real-world scenarios and experiences. Effective strategies for fostering this building block help youth find and evaluate financial information and recognize when they should seek out more information before making financial decisions.

An evidenced-based framework for building financial capability
